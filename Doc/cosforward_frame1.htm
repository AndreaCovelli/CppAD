Cosine Function Forward Taylor Polynomial Theory
If  F(x) = \cos(x)   \[
     1 * F^{(1)} (x) - 0 * F (x)  = - \sin(x)
\]  and in the standard math function differential equation ,  A(x) = 0 ,  B(x) = 1 , and  D(x) = - \sin(x) . We use  a ,  b ,  d ,  s and  z to denote the Taylor coefficients for  A [ X (t) ]  ,  B [ X (t) ] ,  D [ X (t) ]  ,  \sin [ X(t) ] , and  F [ X(t) ]  respectively. It now follows from the general Taylor coefficients recursion formula that for  j = 0 , 1, \ldots ,  \[
\begin{array}{rcl}
z^{(0)} & = & \cos ( x^{(0)} )
\\
e^{(j)} 
& = & d^{(j)} + \sum_{k=0}^{j} a^{(j-k)} * z^{(k)}
\\
& = & -s^{(j)}
\\
z^{(j+1)} & = & \frac{1}{j+1} \frac{1}{ b^{(0)} } 
\left(
     \sum_{k=1}^{j+1} k x^{(k)} e^{(j+1-k)} 
     - \sum_{k=1}^j k z^{(k)}  b^{(j+1-k)} 
\right)
\\
& = & - \frac{1}{j+1} 
     \sum_{k=1}^{j+1} k x^{(k)} s^{(j+1-k)} 
\end{array}
\]  Using  c for the Taylor coefficients of  \cos[ X(t) ] the order  j+1 coefficient can be generated using the lower order coefficients for  \sin[ X(t) ] and  \cos[ X(t) ] by the following formula:  \[
c^{(j+1)} = - \frac{1}{j+1} 
     \sum_{k=1}^{j+1} k x^{(k)} s^{(j+1-k)} 
\]
